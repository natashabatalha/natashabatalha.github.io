A Break Down of the Radiative Transfer Equation

$$I(\tau_i, \mu) = I(\tau_{i+1}, \mu) e^{\delta \tau_i/\mu} - \int_0^{\delta \tau_i} S(\tau',\mu)e^{-\tau'/\mu} d\tau'/\mu.$$

$$\color{#339966} I(\tau_i, \mu) \color{black} = \color{#336699} I(\tau_{i+1}, \mu) e^{\delta \tau_i/\mu} \color{black} - \color{#00BFFF}\int_0^{\delta \tau_i} S(\tau',\mu)e^{-\tau'/\mu} d\tau'/\mu.$$

Integrating the Source Function

We are going to solve the source function integration in two parts.

Part 1: Single Scattering

Single Scattering Component

$$SS=\int_0^{\delta\tau_i}\frac{\omega}{4\pi} F_0P_{single}(\mu, -\mu_0) e^{-\tau'/\mu_0}e^{-\tau'/\mu}d\tau'/\mu$$The \(\tau'\) in \(e^{-\tau'/\mu_0}\) is actually comprised of two terms: 1) the integrated opacity from TOA to the top of the considered layer, and 2) the opacity within the considered layer. Therefore, we first have to replace \(e^{-\tau/\mu_0}\), with \(e^{-(\tau_i + \tau')/\mu_0}\). Now, our \(\tau'\)s in the above equation represent the same value and we can proceed with our integration.

$$=\frac{\omega}{4\pi} F_0P_{single}(\mu, -\mu_0)\int_0^{\delta\tau_i}e^{-(\tau'+\tau_i)/\mu_0}e^{-\tau'/\mu} d\tau'/\mu$$ By integrating, we arrive at ():

$$=\frac{\omega F_0}{4\pi} P_{single}(\mu, -\mu_0) e^{-\tau_i/\mu_0} \frac{\mu_0}{\mu_0 + \mu} \left( 1- e^{-\delta \tau_i\frac{\mu+\mu_0}{\mu\mu_0}} \right)$$

Options for Phase functions (\(P_{single}\)) in PICASO.:

1) One term Henyey-Greenstein(): $$P_{HG}(\cos\Theta) = \frac{1-g^2}{1 + g^2 - 2g\cos(\Theta)}.$$ Not good at capturing backward scattering peak. Compare the two plots as g->1!

2) Default: Two term Henyey-Greenstein(): $$P_{TTHG}(\cos\Theta) = f P_{HG}(\cos\Theta,g_{f}) + (1-f)P_{HG}(\cos\Theta,g_{b}).$$ Better for capturing moderate backward scattering with high forward scattering. We set \(g_f = \bar{g}\) ,\(g_f = -\bar{g}/2\), and \(g_f = 1-g_b^2\). \(\bar{g}\) is calculated from the cloud asymmetry factor and the rayleigh and scattering opacity (see cosb) Note difference at high \(g_f\) below.

3) & 4) TTHG with Rayleigh Phase: $$P_{TTRAY} = \frac{\tau_{cld}}{\tau_{scat}}P_{TTHG} + \frac{\tau_{ray}}{\tau_{scat}}P_{Ray} $$ Where the rayleigh phase function is \( \frac{3}{4}(1+\cos^2(\Theta))\)

Integrating the Source Function

We are going to solve the source function integration in two parts.

Part 2: Multiple Scattering

Multiple Scattering Component

$$MS=\int_0^{\delta\tau_i} e^{-\tau_i/\mu} \int_{-1}^1 \frac{\omega}{2} I(\tau', \mu') P_{multi}(\mu, \mu') d\mu' $$ Solving this term is a bit more complicated because we must integrate over all diffuse angles, \(\mu\). Instead of using the exact TTHG, we have to use simplified approximations. Often, infinite series of suitable orthogonal basis functions can be used (e.g. Legendre or Chebyshev). Currently PICASO only uses Legendre, although it's been shown that at higher orders and high \(g\) Chebyshev polynomials might be more accurate.

Legendre polynomials, allow the \(P_{multi}(\cos\Theta) \approx \sum_{l=0}^{N-1} \beta_lP_l(\cos\Theta)\). Where \(P_l(cos\Theta)\) are the polynomials. \(\beta_l\) are the moments of the phase function. They can be written out as $$\beta_l = \frac{2l + 1}{2} \int_1^{-1} P_l(\cos)p(\cos\Theta)d\cos\Theta$$ This should look familiar, as the asymmetry factor is \(g = \frac{1}{2} \int_1^{-1}P_l(\cos)p(\cos\Theta)d\cos\Theta \). Therefore, the moment can be simplified to \(\beta_l = (2l + 1)g_l \). The first moment is the same asymmetry factor we've been discussing (cosb). The second moment is not as trivial. Below are three ways in which PICASO handles multiple scattering.

Option 1) N=1 Legendre expansion()

With \(\bar{g}\), the N=1 expansion is simply: $$P_{multi}(\cos\Theta) = 1 + 3 \bar{g}\cos\Theta = 1 + 3 \bar{g} \mu\mu'$$ Putting this back into MS creates a simple integral to manipulate.

Option 2) N=2 Legendre expansion()

The calculation of the second moment gets sticky. Many authors Originating from K.Snook's dissertation work. Then later published in Cahoy+2010 leverage the fact that the second order dependence on \(\cos^2\Theta\) is the same as that of Rayleigh scattering (\( \frac{3}{4}(1+\cos^2(\Theta))\)). By forcing \(\beta_l=(2l+1)g_l=g_2\) to yield the Rayleigh phase function in the limit where Rayleigh opacity dominates, we can accurately account for Rayleigh scattering. There we define (gcos2 ) $$ \beta_2=\frac{\tau_{ray}}{2\tau_{scat}} =g_2$$ which tends to 1/2 when \(\tau_{scat}\) is dominated by Rayleigh opacity. The full phase function is then $$P_{multi}(\mu\mu') = 1 + 3\bar{g} \mu\mu' + g_2 \frac{1}{2}\left(3\mu^2 \mu'^2 - 1 \right).$$ Note, the equations that are hyperlinked have been carried through the integration, which we will do on the next slide.

Option 3) N=2 Legendre expansion with \(\delta-Scaled\) Approximation()

As you will see in the next slide, low order (N=1,2) phase functions are not adequate enough to represent high forward scattering, i.e. highly asymmetric scatteres. Although we won't go through it in depth here, one way of making lower order approximations more accurate is to leverage the \(\delta-Eddington\) Approximation. In this approxmation, \(\bar{g}, \tau, \omega\) are all scaled to more accurately capture the forward scattering peak() $$g'=\frac{\bar{g}}{1+\bar{g}}, \tau' = \tau(1-\omega\bar{g}^2), \omega' = \frac{\omega(1-\bar{g}^2)}{1-\omega\bar{g}^2}$$

Integrating the Mutiple Scattering Term

Avert your eyes if you don't care for math. Starting point:

$$MS=\int_0^{\delta\tau_i} e^{-\tau_i/\mu} \int_{-1}^1 \frac{\omega}{2} I(\tau', \mu') \left( 1 + 3\bar{g} \mu\mu' + \frac{g_2}{2} (3\mu^2 \mu'^2 - 1) \right) d\mu' d\tau'/\mu$$ There are three terms in the integration over \(\mu'\). Let's step through them individually.

Term 1 \(= \omega \frac{1}{2}\int_{-1}^1 I(\tau', \mu')d\mu' =\omega \bar{I}(\tau')\)

\( \bar{I}=\frac{1}{2}\int I d\mu'\) is just the mean intensity, which we can use the 2-stream approximation to calculate \( \bar{I}=\frac{I^{+} + I^{-}}{2} =\frac{1}{4\pi\bar{\mu}} (F^+-F^-) \).

Term 2 \(= \omega \frac{3\bar{g}\mu}{2}\int_{-1}^1 \mu' I(\tau', \mu')d\mu' =\omega \frac{1}{2\pi} \frac{3\bar{g}\mu}{2} F(\tau')\) $$= \omega \frac{1}{2\pi} \frac{3\bar{g}\mu}{2}(F^+-F^-)$$

This is because first moment of the intensity field is \(F=2\pi \int I \mu'd\mu'\). In the 2-stream approximation \(F=F^+-F^-\).

Term 3 \(= \omega \frac{g_2}{2}\int_{-1}^1 I(\tau', \mu') (3\mu^2\mu'^2 - 1)d\mu'\)

If we define the second moment of intensity \(I_2 = \int_1^{-1} \mu'^2 I d\mu'\) the solution becomes: \( \frac{\omega g_2}{2} \left( 3\mu^2 I_2(\tau') - \bar{I}(\tau') \right) \). The best way of dealing with this is to create a mean square angle, \(\bar{\mu_2}^2= \int\mu'^2Id\mu'/ \int Id\mu' \). Then we can rewrite Term 3 with just the fluxes: \( = \frac{\omega g_2}{2} (3 \mu^2 \bar{\mu_2}^2 - 1)(\frac{1}{4\pi\bar{\mu}}) (F^+ + F^-) \).

\(MS = \frac{\omega}{2\pi}\int_0^{\delta\tau_i} e^{-\tau_i/\mu} [ F^+(1 + \frac{3\bar{g}\mu}{2} + \frac{g_2}{2}(3 \mu^2 \bar{\mu_2}^2 - 1) ) \) $$ + F^-(1 - \frac{3\bar{g}\mu}{2} + \frac{g_2}{2}(3 \mu^2 \bar{\mu_2}^2 - 1) )]$$

A Comparison of Multiple Scattering Approximations

Takeaway points:

  • At \(g=0\), 2nd order Legendre polynomials approach Rayleigh scattering phase function
  • Approximations are only decent at low \(g=0\) (Note, that in cases where these phase functions have been compared for accuracy they always show \(g\approx0.1\))
  • Delta-Scaling for asymmetry goes as \(g_d=g/(g+1)\), so the forward scattering is reduced to regions where it is in higher agreement with TTHG.
